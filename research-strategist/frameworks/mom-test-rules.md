# Mom Test Rules — For 1:1 Interviews and UT Discussion Guides

Source: Rob Fitzpatrick, *The Mom Test*. These rules apply to **user research** interviews where the goal is to understand current behaviour. They do **not** apply to market-research stated-preference instruments (conjoint, MaxDiff, Van Westendorp) — those have their own validity model.

## The Three Rules

### Rule 1 — Talk about their life, not your idea
- **Do:** Ask about how they currently solve the problem, what tools/apps they use, where it gets painful.
- **Don't:** Pitch the product or the feature. Don't describe a hypothetical solution and ask if they'd use it.
- **Why:** If the participant doesn't know what you're building, they can't lie politely about it.

### Rule 2 — Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future
- **Do:** "When was the last time you [behaviour]?" "Walk me through what happened." "What did you try first?"
- **Don't:** "Would you…", "Do you usually…", "How often do you…", "How would you feel if…"
- **Why:** Past behaviour is fact. Future behaviour is fiction. Generics average across instances and lose detail.

### Rule 3 — Talk less, listen more
- **Do:** Ask the question, then shut up. Use silence. Let them finish their thought.
- **Don't:** Fill silences. Restate the question to make it easier. Suggest answers when they pause.
- **Why:** Every word the researcher speaks is an opportunity to bias. The participant's pause is often where the real thought arrives.

## Question Patterns That Work

| Bad (hypothetical / opinion / leading) | Good (past behaviour / specific / open) |
|---|---|
| Would you use a feature that did X? | Tell me about the last time you tried to do X. |
| Do you like the new homepage? | What did you do the last time you opened the app? |
| How often do you negotiate with sellers? | Walk me through your last successful negotiation. |
| Would you pay for premium? | Have you ever paid for an app subscription? Which one, and why? |
| What features do you want? | What's the most annoying thing about [current workflow]? |
| Is price important to you? | The last time you decided not to buy something on Carousell — why? |
| Would you trust a verified seller more? | The last time you didn't go through with a purchase because you didn't trust the seller — what made you back out? |

## The Three Bad Data Types To Refuse

When you hear these in a transcript or draft script, flag them as low-validity:

1. **Compliments** — "That's a great idea!" Sounds positive, predicts nothing. Politely note it and move on. Never count compliments as validation.
2. **Generics** — "I usually…", "I always…", "People generally…" Average across instances; lose the specific behavioural data.
3. **Future hypotheticals** — "I'd probably…", "I would definitely…", "If you built that, I'd use it." Speculation, not evidence.

## Concrete Commitments — The Real Validation Signal

Words are cheap. The signal that someone actually values what you're researching:
- **Time** — Will they spend an hour with you? Send screenshots? Forward you to a colleague?
- **Reputation** — Will they introduce you to peers? Vouch for you?
- **Money** — Will they pre-order, deposit, sign a letter of intent?

In Carousell context: Has the user already taken action that costs them something? (Posted a listing, made an offer, set up notifications, paid for a bump.) Behavioural commitment is the validation signal — not survey enthusiasm.

## Interview Structure That Bakes In These Rules

```
1. Warm-up (5–10 min)
   - Build rapport. No product topics. Their life, their context.
   - Ends when participant stops giving one-word answers.

2. Past-behaviour core (25–35 min)
   - "Tell me about the last time you [target behaviour]."
   - Ladder down: "What did you do next?" "Why?" "What were you trying to achieve?"
   - 80% of insight comes from this section. Stay here longest.

3. Constraints and workarounds (10 min)
   - "What's the most annoying part?" "What have you tried?" "What did you give up on?"
   - Surfaces unmet needs without pitching.

4. Optional reveal (5 min, only at the very end)
   - If you must discuss your concept, do it last, briefly, and ask "what wouldn't work for you here?"
   - Treat any positive reaction as zero data. Treat negative reactions as informative.

5. Wrap (5 min)
   - "Who else should I talk to about this?" — referrals are commitment.
   - "Can I follow up if I have a question?" — opens diary-study door.
```

## When To Break The Rules

- **Stated-preference market research** (conjoint, MaxDiff, Van Westendorp) — purpose-built to extract preference info; Mom Test rules don't apply.
- **Concept testing for go/no-go pricing** — sometimes you genuinely need stated price reactions; flag the validity caveat in the synthesis.
- **Sensitive topics where past behaviour is hard to recall** — provide memory aids (specific dates, calendar prompts) instead of going hypothetical.

When breaking the rules, name the trade-off in the research plan. Don't pretend the data is more behavioural than it is.
